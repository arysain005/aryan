Compact Heat Exchanger for UAV Cooling
ANSYS FluentSolidWorksCFDAl 6061
↑ 38% efficiency +
What the project is

UAV propulsion motors generate significant heat at sustained high thrust. This project designed a cross-flow microchannel heat exchanger for a 12 kg fixed-wing UAV, replacing a bulky fin array with a compact brazed-aluminium unit integrating directly into the nacelle structure. Goal: reject 180W continuously at 55°C ambient within 80 × 60 × 20mm, under 85g.

What I did
  1. Established heat rejection targets of 180W at 55°C ambient from motor datasheets and field telemetry.
  2. Built a parametric SolidWorks model sweeping channel width (0.4–1.2mm), fin pitch, and wall thickness across 48 geometry variants.
  3. Ran conjugate heat transfer CFD in ANSYS Fluent (Re 1200–4500); identified optimal 0.7mm channel width and 1.1mm fin pitch.
  4. CNC-milled channels in Al 6061, vacuum-brazed headers, bench-tested on a motor dyno with thermocouple array.
  5. Introduced a bifurcated inlet manifold to resolve pressure drop issues — cut ΔP by 29% with only 4% reduction in heat transfer coefficient.
What I learned
  • How to set up conjugate heat transfer simulations and validate against analytical correlations (Dittus-Boelter, within 4%).
  • Pressure drop budgeting must happen early — discovered late, it nearly invalidated the geometry.
  • Parametric CAD + scripted mesh export pipelines dramatically accelerate multi-variant CFD studies.
  • Simulation-to-hardware agreement within 6°C is achievable with careful material property inputs.

Structural Analysis
Topology-Optimised Bicycle Crank Arm
Abaqus FEAAltair OptiStructSLS PrintingNylon PA12
↓ 31% mass +
What the project is

Applied topology optimisation to redistribute material in a road bicycle crank arm only where structurally necessary, then manufactured the organic result via Selective Laser Sintering. Goal: maximum mass reduction without FoS dropping below 3.5.

What I did
  1. Extracted peak pedal forces from a power meter during a maximal sprint — 1,450N downward, 320N lateral, 85Nm torsion.
  2. Meshed the original alloy crank in Abaqus with C3D10 tetra elements (avg 1.8mm) and validated against published strain-gauge data.
  3. Set up OptiStruct topology optimisation with volume fraction 0.65 and minimum member size 3mm (SLS constraint). Ran 42 iterations.
  4. Remodelled the optimised result as a smooth B-rep in SolidWorks; re-ran Abaqus with PA12 properties — FoS ≥ 3.5 confirmed.
  5. Printed at 0.1mm layer height, media-blasted, and fatigue-tested to 100k cycles at 1.5× design load — zero cracking.
What I learned
  • Topology optimisation gives a stress-flow map, not a finished geometry — B-rep reconstruction is a significant skill.
  • SIMP penalisation and volume fraction are sensitive — small changes produce very different material distributions.
  • SLS opens up geometries machining cannot, but minimum member size constraints must be built into the optimisation setup.
  • Stiffness can increase even as mass drops if material is redistributed along principal stress trajectories.

6-DOF Robotic Arm for Precision Assembly
MATLABSolidWorksServo ControlArduino
±0.2mm repeat. +
What the project is

Built a desktop 6-DOF robotic manipulator from scratch — custom-machined aluminium links, hobby servos, and a MATLAB-derived inverse kinematics solver running on an Arduino Mega. Achieved ±0.2mm end-effector repeatability at under £420 total BOM. Designed for PCB component pick-and-place tasks.

What I did
  1. Derived forward kinematics using modified DH parameters in MATLAB Symbolic Toolbox; workspace analysis confirmed 280mm reach with no singularities in the assembly plane.
  2. Designed links as hollow Al 6061 extrusions in SolidWorks; FEA confirmed deflection under 0.15mm at full reach with 200g payload.
  3. Implemented a closed-form IK solver using Pieper's method; Jacobian-based singularity detection with automatic trajectory replanning.
  4. Tuned PID gains using Ziegler-Nichols then refined with MATLAB System Identification; ran gravity compensation from least-squares equilibrium measurements at 20 configurations.
  5. Implemented a backlash compensation algorithm — reducing effective backlash from 0.8° to 0.05°.
What I learned
  • Hobby servos have enough precision for sub-mm work if backlash is actively compensated for in software and mechanically preloaded out of joints.
  • Gravity compensation is not optional — without it, positioning error nearly doubles in extended-reach configurations.
  • Fixed-point arithmetic on Arduino Mega can run a full IK solve at 200Hz if the algorithm avoids floating-point operations.
  • Repeatability and accuracy are distinct — high repeatability but absolute accuracy drifts with temperature.

CFRP Layup Optimisation for Motorsport Panel
NastranComposite FEACFRPAutoclave
↓ 22% shear stress +
What the project is

Optimised the fibre orientation and ply stacking sequence of a T300/epoxy prepreg front splitter to minimise interlaminar shear stress — the dominant failure mode — while reducing ply count and layup time. The panel must resist up to 400N downforce at 130km/h.

What I did
  1. Ran steady RANS CFD (k-ω SST) on the full car geometry to extract pressure distribution at 5 speeds; peak 3.1 kPa at the leading edge.
  2. Modelled the baseline [0/±45/90]s 8-ply layup with Nastran PCOMP cards; found interlaminar shear stress at 14.2 MPa — above the 12 MPa design limit.
  3. Ran a full-factorial Design of Experiments across fibre orientation and ply ordering (81 candidates) using OptiStruct composite optimiser.
  4. Selected [±30/0/90]s sequence; reduced ILS to 11.1 MPa and ply count from 8 to 6.
  5. Manufactured via autoclave at 125°C / 6 bar; UT C-scan confirmed void content 0.8%. Flatness 0.3mm over 600mm span.
What I learned
  • Fibre angle has a non-linear effect on ILS — rotating from 0° to ±30° redistributes in-plane shear between layers counterintuitively.
  • Design of Experiments is far more efficient than manual iteration for composite stacking problems.
  • Layup quality determines whether the FEA prediction holds — manufacturing process control matters as much as design optimisation.
  • CFD-to-structural co-simulation workflows require careful mesh interpolation at the boundary.
