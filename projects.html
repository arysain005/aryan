Thermal Systems
Compact Heat Exchanger for UAV Cooling
ANSYS Fluent SolidWorks CFD Al 6061
↑ 38% efficiency +
What the project is

UAV propulsion motors generate significant heat at sustained high thrust. This project designed a cross-flow microchannel heat exchanger for a 12 kg fixed-wing UAV, replacing a bulky fin array with a compact brazed-aluminium unit that integrates directly into the nacelle structure. The goal was to reject 180W continuously at 55°C ambient within an 80 × 60 × 20mm envelope weighing under 85g.

What I did
  1. Established heat rejection targets from motor datasheets and field telemetry — 180W at 55°C ambient.
  2. Built a parametric SolidWorks model sweeping channel width (0.4–1.2mm), fin pitch, and wall thickness across 48 geometry variants.
  3. Ran conjugate heat transfer CFD in ANSYS Fluent at Re 1200–4500; identified optimal 0.7mm channel width and 1.1mm fin pitch.
  4. CNC-milled channels in Al 6061, vacuum-brazed headers, and bench-tested on a motor dyno with a thermocouple array.
  5. Introduced a bifurcated inlet manifold to resolve a pressure drop issue — cut ΔP by 29% with only a 4% drop in heat transfer coefficient.
What I learned
  • How to set up conjugate heat transfer simulations and validate CFD results against analytical correlations (Dittus-Boelter, within 4%).
  • The importance of pressure drop budgeting early in the design process — discovered late, it nearly invalidated the geometry.
  • Parametric CAD + scripted mesh export pipelines dramatically accelerate multi-variant CFD studies.
  • Simulation-to-hardware agreement within 6°C is achievable with careful material property inputs and boundary condition calibration.

Structural Analysis
Topology-Optimised Bicycle Crank Arm
Abaqus FEA Altair OptiStruct SLS Printing Nylon PA12
↓ 31% mass +
What the project is

Applied topology optimisation to redistribute material in a road bicycle crank arm only where structurally necessary, then manufactured the organic result via Selective Laser Sintering — a process that can realise the complex geometries TO produces, unlike conventional machining. The goal was maximum mass reduction without dropping factor of safety below 3.5.

What I did
  1. Extracted peak pedal forces from a commercial power meter during a maximal sprint — 1,450N downward, 320N lateral, 85Nm torsion.
  2. Meshed the original alloy crank in Abaqus with C3D10 tetra elements (avg 1.8mm) and validated against published strain-gauge data.
  3. Set up OptiStruct topology optimisation with volume fraction 0.65 and minimum member size 3mm to suit SLS process limits. Ran 42 iterations.
  4. Remodelled the optimised result as a smooth B-rep in SolidWorks and re-ran Abaqus with PA12 material properties — FoS ≥ 3.5 confirmed.
  5. Parts printed at 0.1mm layer height, media-blasted, and fatigue-tested to 100k cycles at 1.5× design load — zero cracking.
What I learned
  • Topology optimisation gives a stress-flow map, not a finished geometry — interpretation and B-rep reconstruction is a significant skill in itself.
  • SIMP penalisation and volume fraction are sensitive parameters; small changes produce very different material distributions.
  • SLS opens up geometries machining cannot — but requires minimum member size constraints to be baked into the optimisation setup.
  • Stiffness can increase even as mass drops, if material is redistributed along principal stress trajectories.

Mechatronics
6-DOF Robotic Arm for Precision Assembly
MATLAB SolidWorks Servo Control Arduino
±0.2mm repeat. +
What the project is

Built a desktop 6-DOF robotic manipulator from scratch — custom-machined aluminium links, hobby servos, and a MATLAB-derived inverse kinematics solver running on an Arduino Mega — achieving ±0.2mm end-effector repeatability at a total BOM cost under £420. Designed for PCB component pick-and-place tasks.

What I did
  1. Derived forward kinematics using modified Denavit-Hartenberg parameters in MATLAB Symbolic Toolbox; performed workspace analysis to confirm 280mm reach with no singularities in the assembly plane.
  2. Designed links as hollow Al 6061 extrusions in SolidWorks; FEA confirmed deflection under 0.15mm at full reach with 200g payload.
  3. Implemented a closed-form inverse kinematics solver using Pieper's method; Jacobian-based singularity detection with automatic trajectory replanning.
  4. Tuned PID gains using Ziegler-Nichols then refined with MATLAB System Identification Toolbox; ran gravity compensation from least-squares equilibrium measurements at 20 configurations.
  5. Implemented a backlash compensation algorithm — always approach from same rotational direction — reducing effective backlash from 0.8° to 0.05°.
What I learned
  • Hobby servos have enough precision for sub-mm work if backlash is actively compensated for in software and mechanically preloaded out of joints.
  • Gravity compensation is not optional — without it, positioning error nearly doubles in extended-reach configurations.
  • Fixed-point arithmetic on Arduino Mega can run a full IK solve at 200Hz if the algorithm is carefully structured to avoid floating-point operations.
  • Repeatability and accuracy are distinct — I achieved high repeatability but absolute accuracy still drifts with temperature; learned to design around the more controllable metric.

Materials & Manufacturing
CFRP Layup Optimisation for Motorsport Panel
Nastran Composite FEA CFRP Autoclave
↓ 22% shear stress +
What the project is

Optimised the fibre orientation and ply stacking sequence of a T300/epoxy prepreg front splitter to minimise interlaminar shear stress — the dominant failure mode — while reducing ply count and layup time. The panel must resist up to 400N downforce at 130km/h without aeroelastic distortion that changes diffuser gap height.

What I did
  1. Ran steady RANS CFD (k-ω SST) on the full car geometry to extract pressure distribution over the splitter at 5 speeds; peak 3.1 kPa at the leading edge.
  2. Modelled the baseline [0/±45/90]s 8-ply layup with Nastran PCOMP cards; found interlaminar shear stress 14.2 MPa — above the 12 MPa design limit.
  3. Ran a full-factorial Design of Experiments across fibre orientation and ply ordering (3 factors × 3 levels, 81 candidates) using OptiStruct composite optimiser.
  4. Selected [±30/0/90]s sequence based on minimum interlaminar shear criterion; reduced ILS to 11.1 MPa and ply count from 8 to 6.
  5. Manufactured via autoclave at 125°C / 6 bar; post-cure UT C-scan confirmed void content 0.8% (limit: 2%). Flatness 0.3mm over 600mm span.
What I learned
  • Fibre angle has a non-linear effect on interlaminar shear — rotating from 0° to ±30° redistributes in-plane shear between layers in a way that's counterintuitive at first.
  • Design of Experiments is far more efficient than manual iteration for composite stacking problems with many interacting variables.
  • Layup quality (void content, ply alignment) determines whether the FEA prediction holds in the real part — manufacturing process control is as important as design optimisation.
  • CFD-to-structural co-simulation workflows require careful mesh interpolation at the boundary; a mismatch in coordinate systems cost two days of debugging.
