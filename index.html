Thermal Systems Compact Heat Exchanger for UAV Cooling
ANSYS Fluent SolidWorks CFD Aluminum 6061
↑ 38% efficiency +
Overview

UAV propulsion motors generate significant heat at sustained high thrust — unmanaged thermal load shortens motor life and degrades power output. This project designed a cross-flow microchannel heat exchanger for a 12kg fixed-wing UAV, replacing a bulky fin array with a compact brazed-aluminium unit that integrates directly into the nacelle structure.

Design Process
Thermal Requirement Analysis

Established heat rejection target of 180W continuous at 55°C ambient from motor datasheet and field telemetry. Defined mass budget of ≤ 85g and envelope of 80 × 60 × 20mm.

Parametric CAD Modelling

Built parametric SolidWorks model sweeping channel width (0.4–1.2mm), fin pitch, and wall thickness. Generated 48 geometry variants exported to ANSYS Fluent mesh pipeline via scripted SolidWorks API calls.

CFD Simulation (ANSYS Fluent)

Ran conjugate heat transfer simulations at Re 1200–4500. Identified optimal 0.7mm channel width and 1.1mm fin pitch; confirmed laminar-to-turbulent transition behaviour consistent with Dittus-Boelter correlation within 4%.

Prototype Manufacture & Test

CNC-milled channels in Al 6061, vacuum-brazed headers. Bench-tested on a motor dyno with thermocouple array. Simulation-to-hardware agreement within 6°C across all load points.

Challenges

Pressure drop across the microchannel array initially exceeded the coolant pump head by 18%. Resolved by introducing a bifurcated inlet manifold that distributes flow evenly across two parallel channel banks, cutting pressure drop by 29% with only a 4% reduction in heat transfer coefficient.

Structural Analysis Topology-Optimised Bicycle Crank Arm
Abaqus FEA Altair OptiStruct SLS Printing Nylon PA12
↓ 31% mass +
Overview

Road cycling cranks are loaded under complex multi-axis bending during sprinting and climbing. The goal was to apply topology optimisation to redistribute material only where it is structurally necessary, then manufacture the result via Selective Laser Sintering — a process that can realise the organic geometries TO generates, unlike conventional machining.

Design Process
Load Case Definition

Extracted pedal forces from a commercial power meter during a maximal sprint: peak 1,450N downward, 320N lateral, 85Nm torsion about the spindle. Three load cases combined for worst-case envelope.

Baseline FEA (Abaqus)

Meshed original alloy crank with C3D10 tetra elements (avg 1.8mm), validated against published strain-gauge data. Identified over-stressed regions near spindle bore and lightly-loaded mid-section — classic mass-penalty zone.

Topology Optimisation (OptiStruct)

Set volume fraction target 0.65 with minimum member size 3mm (SLS process constraint). SIMP penalisation factor p=3. Ran 42 iterations to convergence. Interpreted isosurface at 0.3 density threshold.

Geometry Reconstruction & Validation

Remodelled TO result as smooth B-rep in SolidWorks using guide curves from optimised topology. Re-ran Abaqus with PA12 material card (E=1.75GPa). FoS ≥ 3.5 confirmed across all load cases.

SLS Manufacture & Physical Testing

Parts printed at 0.1mm layer height, post-processed with media blasting. Static load tested to 3× design load on a materials testing frame. No failure to 4,350N. Fatigue cycling to 100k cycles at 1.5× load — zero cracking.

Key Finding

The optimised geometry relocated material from the mid-arm web into two load-bearing ribs aligned with principal stress trajectories. This biologically-inspired strut pattern achieved superior stiffness-to-weight compared to the conventional ribbed extrusion — stiffness actually increased by 8% despite the mass reduction.

Mechatronics 6-DOF Robotic Arm for Precision Assembly
MATLAB SolidWorks Servo Control Arduino
±0.2mm repeatability +
Overview

Desktop robotic arms for PCB assembly typically cost £8k–40k. This project built a desktop 6-DOF manipulator from scratch — custom machined links, hobbyist servos driven by a custom torque-compensation controller — achieving ±0.2mm end-effector repeatability at a total BOM cost under £420.

Design Process
Kinematic Design & DH Parameters

Chose modified Denavit-Hartenberg convention for 6R configuration. Derived forward kinematics symbolically in MATLAB Symbolic Toolbox. Workspace analysis confirmed 280mm reach sphere with no singular configurations in the assembly plane.

Structural Design (SolidWorks)

Links modelled as hollow Al 6061 extrusions with servo pockets. FEA confirmed deflection under 0.15mm at full reach with 200g payload — within positioning budget. Joints use dual angular contact bearings preloaded to eliminate backlash.

Inverse Kinematics Solver

Implemented closed-form IK using Pieper's method for the wrist-sphere decoupling. Jacobian-based singularity detection with automatic joint-space trajectory replanning. Solver runs at 200Hz on Arduino Mega with fixed-point arithmetic.

Controller Tuning & Calibration

Gravity compensation torque model identified via least-squares from static equilibrium measurements at 20 configurations. PID gains tuned with Ziegler-Nichols, then refined with MATLAB System Identification Toolbox. Repeatability measured over 500 pick-and-place cycles with a laser displacement sensor.

Key Challenge

Hobby servo backlash (up to 0.8°) dominated positioning error. Mitigated by implementing a backlash compensation algorithm that always approaches a target from the same rotational direction, and by preloading joint bearings against a compliant spring element — reducing effective backlash to under 0.05°.

Materials & Manufacturing CFRP Layup Optimisation for Motorsport Panel
Nastran Composite FEA CFRP Autoclave
↓ 22% shear stress +
Overview

Carbon fibre front splitters must resist downforce-induced bending (up to 400N at 130km/h) while remaining stiff enough to avoid aeroelastic distortion that changes diffuser gap height. This project optimised the ply stacking sequence and fibre orientation of a T300/epoxy prepreg splitter to minimise interlaminar shear stress — the dominant failure mode — while reducing layup complexity.

Design Process
Aero Load Quantification

CFD (steady RANS, k-ω SST) on full car geometry extracted pressure distribution over splitter at 5 speeds. Peak surface pressure 3.1 kPa at leading edge; dominant bending span 420mm. Results imported as distributed pressure loads in Nastran.

Baseline Composite FEA

Modelled existing [0/±45/90]s 8-ply layup with PCOMP cards. Tsai-Wu failure index peaked at 0.71 at the mounting tab interface. Interlaminar shear stress 14.2 MPa — above the 12 MPa design limit for co-cured joints.

Stacking Sequence Optimisation

Ran a Design of Experiments (full factorial, 3 factors × 3 levels) across orientation and ply ordering using OptiStruct composite optimiser. Symmetry and balance constraints enforced. Selected [±30/0/90]s sequence from 81 candidates based on min ILS criterion.

Autoclave Manufacture & Inspection

Prepreg cut on CNC ply cutter; laid up on CNC-machined aluminium tool. Autoclave cure at 125°C / 6 bar per manufacturer data sheet. Post-cure UT C-scan showed void content 0.8% — well within 2% aerospace limit. Flatness measured at 0.3mm over 600mm span.

Key Finding

Rotating the primary 0° plies to ±30° redistributes in-plane shear more evenly between layers, reducing the interlaminar peel force at the tab interface by 22%. The change also reduced ply count from 8 to 6 without exceeding the deflection limit — cutting layup time by ~35 minutes per part.
